# Growth & Marketing Reference Guide

## Foundation 1: Use Case Map

### The Seven-Field Use Case Map
The foundation for all growth strategy starts with deep understanding of how customers use your product.

**Field 1: Problem**
- What specific pain or inefficiency does the customer experience?
- Articulate in customer language, not product terms
- Example: "Sales reps waste time manually updating CRM after each call"

**Field 2: Use Cases**
- Specific jobs customers accomplish with your product
- Multiple use cases may exist within same customer segment
- Example: "Call logging", "Meeting notes summarization", "CRM data enrichment"

**Field 3: Alternatives**
- What customers do today instead of using your product
- Could be manual, competitor, DIY, or do-nothing
- Example: Manual note-taking, spreadsheets, pen and paper, not tracking at all

**Field 4: Why Choose Us**
- Distinct advantages vs. alternatives
- Could be speed, accuracy, cost, ease of use, integrations
- Grounded in measurable benefit or unique capability
- Example: "60% faster than manual entry, integrates with Salesforce, requires no setup"

**Field 5: Target User/ICP**
- Who specifically has this problem and would value the solution?
- Define by role, company size, industry, workflow characteristics
- Example: "Enterprise sales directors at mid-market B2B SaaS companies"

**Field 6: Frequency**
- How often does this use case occur?
- Daily, weekly, monthly, or episodic?
- Informs how important this use case is to overall product value
- Example: "Sales reps make 5-10 calls daily; this impacts their entire workflow"

**Field 7: Consideration Time**
- How long does customer deliberation typically take?
- Days, weeks, months, or immediate?
- Affects sales cycle length and education requirements
- Example: "2-week evaluation period with multiple stakeholders"

### Anti-User/Customer Profile
- Who should NOT be your customer?
- What characteristics indicate poor fit?
- Helps avoid wasting resources on unlikely conversions
- Example: "Solo freelancers with < 1 call per day (use case isn't frequent enough)"

### Anchor on Use Case, Not Persona
**Why This Matters**:
- Personas can be generic; use cases are specific and actionable
- Same persona may have different use cases with different priorities
- Growth strategies should address specific use cases, not broad personas
- Example: A "VP of Sales" may primarily care about team productivity, while a "Sales Rep" cares about time savings per activity

## Foundation 2: Growth Model

### Habit Loops: Trigger → Action → Reward
The mechanics of how users become engaged, habitual users of your product

**Trigger**: What prompts user action?
- **Organic Trigger**: Intrinsic motivation ("I want to log this call")
- **Manufactured Trigger**: Product-created ("We'll remind you to log calls")
- **Environmental Trigger**: External circumstance ("Meeting just ended, time to update CRM")

**Action**: What does user do?
- Lowest effort path to getting value
- Should be frictionless; every click or step reduces completion rate

**Reward**: What does user get?
- Immediate, tangible benefit
- Primary reward (time saved, status, data insight) should align with why they're here

### Acquisition Loops: How You Acquire Users

**Viral Loops**:
- **Personal Viral**: Users invite friends (Dropbox, Gmail, Slack)
- **Financial Viral**: Incentive for referrals (Uber, Robinhood)
- **Social Viral**: Public sharing or network effects (TikTok, Twitter, Figma)

**Content Loops**:
- **User-Generated Content (UGC) × User Distributed**: Users create content, their network discovers it
- **User-Generated Content × Company Distributed**: Users create, you amplify to broader audience
- **Company-Generated Content × User Distributed**: You create, users share within their networks

**Paid Loops**:
- **Ad Networks**: Facebook, Google, LinkedIn ads driving to landing page
- **Sales Motion**: Sales team calling, emailing, meeting with prospects

### Linear Sources as "Principal" Feeding Loops
- **Linear Acquisition**: Direct traffic, organic search, partnerships
- These may not create viral multipliers but provide predictable, foundational user flow
- Often most reliable for mature companies
- Example: "Most B2B SaaS relies on Google search for 30%+ of MQLs"

### Loop vs. Linear Evaluation Criteria
|Criteria|Loop (Viral/Network)|Linear (Paid/Content)|
|---|---|---|
|**Scalability**|Increases with user base|Increases with budget|
|**Unit Economics**|Improves as network grows|Typically fixed or declining|
|**Predictability**|Harder to predict/control|More predictable and controllable|
|**Time to Scale**|Exponential but slower to start|Linear and faster to start|
|**Best for**|Consumer, B2B2C, networks|Enterprise, high LTV|
|**Strategic Role**|Primary growth engine|Foundation + paid acceleration|

## Monetization Model & Friction Analysis

### Four Components of Monetization Model

**1. Price Scaling**
- How does price increase with customer value received?
- Per-seat, per-transaction, per-unit, flat-rate?
- Volume discounts or usage-based tiers?

**2. Features**
- Which features are included in each tier?
- What's held back for premium tiers?
- How does tier match customer's use case?

**3. Amount**
- What's the price point?
- Competitive analysis and value-based pricing
- Customer willingness to pay research

**4. Timing**
- When do you capture payment?
- Free trial before commitment?
- Annual vs. monthly billing impact on cohort retention

### Friction Spectrum & Channel Implications
**High Friction → Low Friction**

- **Sales-assisted** (Enterprise): Highest friction but highest deal size
- **Sales-light** (Mid-market): Medium friction, medium deal size
- **Self-serve** (SMB/Consumer): Low friction, high volume, low deal size

**Implication**: Your monetization model affects which acquisition channels work

## Growth Marketing Strategy

### Wrong Approach 1: Spaghetti Strategy
- Testing everything simultaneously without hypothesis
- Results: Scattered resources, unclear learnings, no compound progress
- Common in early-stage teams without strategic direction
- Leads to local optimization without systemic improvement

### Wrong Approach 2: Optimization Mindset
- Focused entirely on local maxima (incrementally improving existing channels)
- Results: 5-10% improvements annually, missing 10X opportunities
- Misses major channel shifts or market changes
- Classic trap: optimizing dying channels instead of experimenting with new ones

### Right Approach: Foundational Understanding → Strategic Decisions
1. **Deep Dive**: Understand use cases, customer segments, and core value prop
2. **Channel Fit Analysis**: Which acquisition channels align with your customer and use case?
3. **Strategic Bets**: Pick 2-3 channels to go deep on, not surface-level on all
4. **Systematic Testing**: Within chosen channels, test systematically and measure learning
5. **Compound**: Build momentum in channels that work; sunset channels that don't

**Key Principle**: Strategy (which channels) before tactics (optimization within channel)

## Paywall Conversion Optimization

### Key Metric: Paywall View Rate Targets
Define targets based on:
- **Current State**: What % of users are seeing the paywall?
- **Benchmarks**: How does this compare to competitors or industry?
- **Target**: Where do you want to be in 6/12 months?
- **Example**: Drive paywall views from 15% to 30% of active users

### Paywall Placement Strategy
- **Trigger-based**: Show paywall when user has experienced core value
- **Time-based**: Show after N days or after completing onboarding
- **Usage-based**: Show after user has hit a meaningful usage threshold
- **Conversion rate by placement**: Measure which timing delivers best conversion

### Paywall Visuals & Copy Optimization
- **Clear Value Prop**: Why should they upgrade? What extra capability?
- **Social Proof**: How many companies use this tier? What results?
- **Urgency**: Limited-time offer? Upcoming price increase?
- **Test Variations**: A/B test headline, images, benefits listed, CTA button

### Systematic Testing & Recurring Meetings
- **Monthly A/B Testing Calendar**: Schedule 2-3 experiments monthly
- **Weekly Experiment Reviews**: Tuesday morning sync on results
- **Metric Dashboard**: View all paywall metrics in one place
- **Hypothesis Tracking**: Document what you expect to learn, not just what happened
- **Iteration Playbook**: When to keep testing, when to call a winner, when to move on

## New User Onboarding

### Emphasizing the Primary Value Proposition
- **Identify**: What's the ONE main benefit that made them sign up?
- **Emphasize**: Onboarding should clearly demonstrate this benefit in first 5 minutes
- **Eliminate**: Remove secondary features, advanced options, edge cases from initial flow
- **Measure**: Does onboarding completion predict long-term engagement and retention?

### Calculate "Psychologically Perceived Score"
- How quickly does user feel value from the product?
- Directly impacts whether they'll come back for second use
- **Measure via**: Post-onboarding survey ("How much value did you get?"), Session return rate
- **Target**: Users should feel meaningful value within their first 5-10 minutes in product

### Define Activation Metric Through Multiple Methods

**Quantitative Analysis**:
- Cohort analysis: Which in-product behaviors predict 30-day retention?
- Regression analysis: What's the strongest leading indicator of retention?
- Example: Users who log 3+ entries within first week retain at 65%; those who don't retain at 15%

**Qualitative Feedback**:
- Customer interviews: "What moment made you realize this product would help you?"
- Support tickets: What's the gap between expectation and actual product?
- Activation is often a specific "aha moment" they can articulate

**Surveys**:
- Post-activation: "What was your first valuable use of this product?"
- Churn interviews: "When did you first lose interest?"
- Pattern recognition: What actions/moments correlate to continued use?

**Define Activation**:
- Specific behavior(s) that predict retention
- Measurable and trackable in analytics
- Achievable within reasonable onboarding timeframe
- Example: "User creates 3+ projects within first 7 days"

### Maximize Activation Rate: Step-by-Step Conversion Optimization
**Phase 1: Baseline Measurement**
- Current activation rate (% of signups hitting activation metric)
- Conversion rate at each step of onboarding
- Identify the biggest drop-off point

**Phase 2: Address Biggest Friction**
- If 60% drop off at "invite team members" step, focus there first
- Either make step easier or remove it from critical path
- Measure impact on activation rate

**Phase 3: Guided Optimization**
- Month 1: Reduce complexity (fewer steps, clearer language)
- Month 2: Improve clarity (better tooltips, examples, defaults)
- Month 3: Add delight (progress bar, encouragement, progress celebration)
- Month 4: Personalize (different flows for different user types)

**Phase 4: Validation & Iteration**
- Measure activation rate weekly
- Run A/B tests on top 2-3 friction points
- Document what works; build playbook for future onboarding improvements
- Typical uplift: 15-30% improvement in activation rate over 8-12 weeks

### Onboarding Metrics Dashboard
- **Signups**: New users entering onboarding
- **Step Completion Rates**: % completing each step
- **Activation Rate**: % reaching activation metric
- **Time to Activation**: Days/hours from signup to activation
- **Retention at 7/30/90 days**: Cohort retention curve
- **NPS for Onboarded Users**: Satisfaction with initial experience

### Onboarding Decision Gates
- **Gate 1 (Day 1)**: Did user feel any value? (Return rate, usage metrics)
- **Gate 2 (Day 7)**: Is user activated per definition? (Metric tracking)
- **Gate 3 (Day 30)**: Are activated users retained? (Cohort retention)

**If gates fail**: Iterate onboarding. If gates pass: Move focus to engagement and retention loops.
