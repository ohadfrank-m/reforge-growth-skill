# PLG Foundations Reference Guide

A comprehensive reference for Product-Led Growth frameworks from Reforge courses.

---

## 1. What is Product-Led Growth (PLG)

### Definition & Core Concept

**Product-Led Growth** means the product itself acquires, activates, engages, or monetizes users without requiring direct human intervention.

**Key Principle:** PLG is not all-or-nothing; it's about layering product-led tactics strategically across your growth model.

### Six Key Traits for PLG Readiness

| Trait | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Short Time-to-Value** | Users realize core value within minutes/hours of signing up, not days |
| **Self-Serve Growth Model** | Users can progress through the product without sales/CS support |
| **Distribution Baked into Product** | Growth happens through product usage (viral loops, sharing) |
| **Multiplayer Mode/Collaboration** | Product enables collaboration; users invite others |
| **Self-Serve Monetization** | Freemium features or one-click upgrades without sales contact |
| **Freemium Offering** | Free tier or trial allows product experience before payment |

## 2. Growth Motions Framework

### Three Core Growth Motion Types

| Motion | Primary Driver | Characteristics |
|--------|---|---|
| **Product-Led Growth** | Product features, virality, self-service | Users drive own activation; minimal sales involvement |
| **Sales-Led Growth** | Sales & CS teams | Direct engagement for acquisition and activation |
| **Marketing-Led Growth** | Content, campaigns, brand | Drives awareness and initial engagement |

### Growth Motion Spectrum

PLG exists on a spectrum with four primary positions:

```
Product-Led ──> Product-Led + Sales-Assisted ──> Sales-Led + Product-Assisted ──> Sales-Led
```

**Position 1: Product-Led**
- Users activate fully through product experience
- No sales/CS touchpoints

**Position 2: Product-Led + Sales-Assisted**
- Product enables self-serve activation
- Sales team assists with expansion deals

**Position 3: Sales-Led + Product-Assisted**
- Sales team drives acquisition
- Product supports retention and engagement

**Position 4: Sales-Led**
- Sales team controls entire customer journey
- Product primarily for usage/retention

### 3×3 Growth Motions Matrix

Combine growth motion type (rows) with customer lifecycle stage (columns):

|  | Acquisition | Activation/Engagement | Retention/Monetization |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Product-Led** | Viral loops, freemium trials | Self-service onboarding, in-app guidance | Habit loops, self-serve upgrade paths |
| **Marketing-Led** | Content, SEO, campaigns | Educational content, webinars | Email campaigns, community |
| **Sales-Led** | Outbound sales, partnerships | Sales calls, custom demos | Account management, upsells |

### Real-World Evolution Examples

**Lyft Growth Motion Shift:**
- Started Product-Led: Referral loops, simple sign-up, in-app growth
- Evolved to Sales-Led + Product-Assisted: Enterprise contracts for corporate programs

**Miro B2C to B2B Transition:**
- B2C: Product-Led (freemium, viral invitations, self-serve)
- B2B: Product-Led + Sales-Assisted (self-serve signup + sales for expansion)

## 3. B2B Growth Hypothesis Framework

### Four Parts of Growth Model

Every growth model consists of four linked components:

| Component | Definition | Questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition** | How users discover and sign up | What brings users to the product? |
| **Activation** | How users experience initial value | Does new user experience core value quickly? |
| **Engagement** | How users form usage habits | Do users return repeatedly? |
| **Monetization** | How users convert to revenue | When/how do users pay? |

*These create a loop, not a funnel. Success in one component feeds the next.*

### Four Key Factors Determining Growth Motion

These factors dictate which growth motion works best:

| Factor | Description | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer WTP (Willingness to Pay)** | How much value users perceive | Higher WTP → Sales-led makes sense |
| **Customer Motivation** | Why users need your product | Urgent need → Sales works; exploratory → Product-Led works |
| **Customer Ability** | Can users adopt without help? | High ability → Product-Led works |
| **Customer Permission** | Can you reach users? (budget access, etc.) | Direct budget access → Sales works |

### Buyer vs. User Personas

**Critical Distinction:**

| Aspect | Buyer Persona | User Persona |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Person who approves/pays | Person who uses product daily |
| **Growth Motion Impact** | Determines monetization approach | Determines acquisition & engagement |
| **Example** | CFO approving Slack purchase | Team member using Slack daily |

In PLG, the **user often becomes the buyer** (bottom-up motion).

### Use Case Identification Process

**Step 1: Identify Customer Segments**
- Who are your target customers? (Company size, industry, role)

**Step 2: Map Use Cases**
- What problems does each segment face?
- What outcomes do they want?

**Step 3: Prioritize by Demand Source**

| Demand Source | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Bottom-Up Demand** | Users discover product, show value, buying interest emerges |
| **Company Strategy** | Leadership decides target market; product built accordingly |

**PLG Insight:** Bottom-up demand often outperforms planned strategy. Users self-select for highest-fit use cases.

## 4. PLG Direct & Indirect Benefits

### Direct Benefits (Measurable & Immediate)

| Benefit | How It Works |
|---|---|
| **Scales Without Linear Cost Increase** | Each new user doesn't require new sales rep; CAC grows slower than revenue |
| **More Efficient Marketing Spend** | Product itself drives retention/expansion; CAC improves over time |
| **Offensive Market Moves** | Expand into new segments/geographies with same product |
| **Defensive Market Moves** | Low switching costs; capture users before competitors reach them |

### Indirect Benefits (Strategic & Compound)

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Higher Willingness-to-Pay (WTP)** | Users experienced value before paying; perceive higher ROI |
| **Network Effects** | More users → more collaboration → more value → more users (viral) |
| **Adjacent Customer Acquisition** | One user type invites related user types (e.g., designer invites developer) |
| **Better Product Insights** | Usage data reveals what features create value; guides roadmap |
| **Shorter Sales Cycles** | Expansion deals close faster when users already understand product |

## 5. Growth Model Design

### Habit Loop Framework (Engagement Motor)

Create repeating usage through the habit loop:

```
Trigger → Action → Reward → Habit
```

| Stage | Definition | Example (Slack) |
|---|---|---|
| **Trigger** | Internal or external cue to use product | New message notification |
| **Action** | Behavior user performs | Open Slack, read message |
| **Reward** | Payoff user receives | Social connection, resolved question |
| **Repeat** | Loop reinforces habit | User returns daily |

**PLG Application:** Strong habit loops create retention without needing constant marketing.

### Acquisition Loops Framework

Build sustainable acquisition through loops, not one-time campaigns:

| Loop Type | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Viral Loop** | Each user invites others through product | Slack invite → new member |
| **Content Loop** | User-generated content drives discovery | YouTube creator attracts viewers |
| **Paid Loop** | Sustainable paid acquisition feeding free tier | Facebook ads → free trial conversion |
| **Sales Loop** | Expansion deals, partnerships | Enterprise deal → new use cases |

### Loop vs. Funnel Thinking

**Funnel Model (Linear):**
```
Awareness → Signup → Activation → Engagement → Monetization
              (leak at each stage)
```

**Problem:** Loses users at every step; requires constant new traffic.

**Loop Model (Compounding):**
```
Acquisition Loop → Activation Loop ↔ Engagement Loop → Monetization Loop
    ↑_________________________(Expansion/Viral Feedback)_________________↓
```

**Advantage:** Engaged users drive acquisition; loops reinforce each other.

### Growth Multiplier Metric

Measure loop health with the **Viral Coefficient** (V):

```
Growth Multiplier = 1 / (1 - V)
```

Where V = number of new users acquired per existing user

| V | Multiplier | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| 0.0 | 1x | No viral growth (linear acquisition only) |
| 0.25 | 1.33x | Modest viral amplification |
| 0.5 | 2x | Each user brings one additional user over lifetime |
| 1.0 | ∞ | Exponential growth (unsustainable without constraints) |

**PLG Strategy:** Optimize loops to maximize viral coefficient without diluting quality.

### Principal vs. Dependent Loops

**Principal Loops (Linear Sources):**
- Paid acquisition, organic search, content
- Bring new users into system
- Feed other loops

**Dependent Loops (Multiplier Effects):**
- Viral, referral, expansion
- Multiply principal loop effectiveness
- Don't work without principal source

## 6. Monetization Model Friction Analysis

### Four Components of Monetization Model

Every monetization decision involves these four levers:

| Component | Questions |
|---|---|
| **How Price Scales** | Does price scale per user, per company, per feature, per usage? |
| **What Features** | Which features are free vs. paid? |
| **How Much** | What's the pricing tier structure? |
| **When Charged** | Freemium vs. trial vs. immediate payment? |

### Friction Spectrum

Monetization adds friction; position friction based on acquisition channel:

```
Low Friction ← → High Friction
Free → Freemium → Trial → Pay Upfront → Sales Required
```

| Friction Level | Model | When to Use | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Very Low** | Free + Ads | Max user acquisition | No direct revenue |
| **Low** | Freemium | Self-serve B2B, viral adoption | Harder to monetize |
| **Medium** | Free Trial | Premium product, high WTP | Conversion rate risk |
| **High** | Pay Upfront | Enterprise SaaS | Reduces acquisition volume |
| **Very High** | Sales Required | Complex/expensive products | Requires sales team |

### Friction Placement Strategy

Strategically place monetization friction at different points in growth loops:

| Loop Stage | Friction Approach | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition Loop** | Low friction (freemium) | Offer free tier to enable viral signup |
| **Activation Loop** | Low friction (time-unlimited) | Free tier works until user hits limits |
| **Engagement Loop** | Medium friction (soft wall) | Free users see "upgrade for team access" |
| **Monetization Loop** | High friction (pricing call) | Enterprise requires sales conversation |

### Growth Motion & Monetization Alignment

| Growth Motion | Best Monetization Model | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Product-Led** | Freemium or free trial | Users activate without sales; monetize once proven value |
| **Product-Led + Sales-Assisted** | Freemium + transparent pricing | Free layer builds demand; sales closes expansion deals |
| **Sales-Led + Product-Assisted** | Trial or pay-upfront | Sales drives adoption; friction is acceptable |
| **Sales-Led** | Custom pricing / pay-upfront | Sales team handles entire negotiation |

### Monetization Design Process

1. **Identify Target User Value Perception**
   - What are users willing to pay based on outcomes?

2. **Map Feature Value**
   - Which features drive highest perceived value?
   - What can be free? What must be paid?

3. **Choose Pricing Model**
   - Seats (per user), Usage (per action), Feature (per tier), Company (per customer)

4. **Select Friction Level**
   - Match to growth motion and acquisition channel

5. **Test & Iterate**
   - Measure conversion rates at each friction point
   - Optimize based on customer feedback and willingness-to-pay data

## 7. PLG-Specific Activation: Team-Level Metrics

### The PLG Difference: Monetizable Unit Is the Team

A major pitfall in PLG is focusing too heavily on *individual* activation metrics. This creates blind spots because the monetizable unit in PLG is usually the **team**, not the individual user.

Individual activation is necessary but not sufficient. A product can have hundreds of individually-activated users at a company and still fail to convert that account — because no team has formed a shared workflow around the product.

**Critical distinction:**

| Metric Type | What It Tracks | Risk If Over-Indexed |
|---|---|---|
| Individual Activation | % of users who reach Setup/Aha/Habit | Optimizes for users who activate in isolation; misses team adoption |
| Team-Level Activation | % of accounts where a team reaches activation together | Tracks the actual monetizable behavior |

**Example — SurveyMonkey:** Had 800 individually-activated free users at one company. But no enterprise conversion happened because individual users were using the product independently, not as a team workflow. The individual activation metric looked healthy; the account was going nowhere.

---

### Three Pillars of PLG Team-Level Activation

#### Pillar A: Determine Your Ideal Team-Level Activation Metrics

Team-level activation does **not** require the product to have mandatory multiplayer features. Value realization can happen even when users aren't collaborating directly — if a team's aggregate usage delivers an outcome the team depends on.

Ask: *"What does it look like when a team has formed a habit around this product?"*

| Signal Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Co-creation** | Multiple users actively building together in the same product artifact | Miro board with 3+ active collaborators |
| **Dependent workflows** | Users who don't co-create, but whose work flows from one user to the next | Amplitude: analyst creates report → PM reads it → decision made |
| **Shared outcomes** | Team collectively realizes a business outcome, even without direct interaction | Veed: content team produces videos → manager reviews performance in same dashboard |

**Multiplayer is not mandatory.** The test is: *"Does the team's business outcome depend on this product in a way that no one person can simply cancel without affecting others?"*

#### Pillar B: Measure Activation Across Different Account Roles

Within any account, different types of users activate in different ways. Measure them separately:

| User Type | Definition | Relevant Activation Metric |
|---|---|---|
| **PQA (Product-Qualified Account)** | Individual user behavior aggregated to the account level — indicates the account has enough individual value realization to be worth sales attention | Threshold: e.g., ≥3 users reach individual Aha Moment in same account |
| **Team Activation** | Account-level metric tracking whether a team has formed shared usage patterns | Threshold: e.g., 2+ users creating/sharing artifacts with each other within 14 days |
| **Economic Buyer** | Decision-maker who approves payment — may never use the product themselves | Reached via sales or marketing, not product activation |
| **Influencer / Champion** | Power user inside the account who drives internal adoption | Track: Has this user invited ≥2 teammates? |

**PQA vs. Team Activation are separate metrics.** A PQA signals "this account has enough individual usage to be worth a sales conversation." Team Activation signals "this account has a shared habit that creates lock-in."

**Case studies:**
- **Veed:** Switched from measuring monthly individual activations to weekly *team* activations. The change revealed a cohort of accounts where multiple users were active but no team activation was happening — leading to a targeted onboarding intervention to prompt sharing.
- **Miro:** Built team-focused activation from the start. Their Aha Moment was specifically designed as a team event (shared board with 3+ collaborators), not an individual event.
- **Amplitude:** Non-co-creation product — analysts create reports, but PMs and executives are the consumers. Team activation = "at least one analyst and one PM/exec both actively used the same dashboard in the last 14 days." Tracking this revealed that accounts where only analysts were active churned at 3× the rate.

#### Pillar C: Revisit and Adjust Activation Flows Over Time

Activation metrics are not permanent. As the product evolves and user behavior shifts, the correlation between early actions and long-term retention changes. Build a cadence to revisit:

1. **Quarterly re-run of the activation correlation matrix** — test whether the current Setup/Aha/Habit thresholds still have strong PPV/NPV against current cohorts
2. **Expand to new user types** — as new personas adopt the product (e.g., new roles, new company sizes), re-test activation for those segments; the Aha Moment may differ
3. **Track team activation separately by account tier** — SMB, Mid-Market, and Enterprise may have different team activation dynamics; don't apply one threshold to all

**Warning signs that activation metrics need recalibration:**
- Overall activation rate improves, but 6-month retention doesn't follow
- New cohorts activate at the same rate as old ones, but churn faster after Month 2
- Sales team reports accounts where "everyone loves the product" but conversion stalls

### PLG Activation Checklist (Team-Level)

- [ ] Have you defined a team-level activation metric (not just individual)?
- [ ] Does your team activation metric reflect shared value realization, not just co-presence in the product?
- [ ] Do you track PQAs separately from team activation?
- [ ] Do you have activation metrics for each distinct user type in the account (creator, consumer, admin, economic buyer)?
- [ ] Do you have a quarterly cadence to re-test activation correlations?
- [ ] Has your team activation metric been validated against 6-month account retention?

## Summary: PLG Readiness Checklist

Before implementing PLG:

- [ ] Can users experience core value in < 1 hour?
- [ ] Do users activate without human intervention (docs, onboarding, UI)?
- [ ] Does product usage naturally lead to user growth (viral/referral)?
- [ ] Can users accomplish use cases in self-serve manner?
- [ ] Is there a clear path to paid (freemium, upsell, expansion)?
- [ ] Do you have product analytics to measure loops?
- [ ] Does growth motion (Product/Marketing/Sales) align with customer factors?

**Success Indicator:** Paid customers predominantly come from self-activated free users, not sales outreach.
